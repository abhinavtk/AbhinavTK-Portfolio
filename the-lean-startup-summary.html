The Lean Startup

Summary

May 29, 2021

The Lean Startup by Eric Ries is an influential book in the startup ecosystem. The book has helped many startups to become big businesses by applying the lessons and principles of the book. Eric Ries being a co-founder and worked at chief managerial positions at various startups has written this book based on real-world experiences. The book can be structured into 3 sections namely, Vision, Steer and Accelerate.

Vision

The inspiration for the Lean Startup model has to be attributed to the Toyota manufacturing revolution. They made Toyota into a multinational company by implementing the following principles:

  • Leveraging the knowledge and skills of individuals in the company.
  • Reducing batch sizes.
  • Make use of just-in-time production and managing inventory.
  • Accelerated cycle times.
This model showed the importance of value-generating behaviour, which is one of the core principles of the Lean Startup model. The author, therefore, says that a startup becomes successful by having the right execution, that is to follow the right process and not that of having a great idea or delivering the product at the right time. Hence the Lean Startup model is based on the following:
  • Fast cycle times.
  • Focus on what the customer wants.
  • Using scientific and statistical approach for decision making.
The lean startup business model is hence a new approach for developing new products in innovative ways like speedy iterations, customer insights, creative vision and ambitions.

The concept of validated learning is introduced in the book. This concept is a quick and accurate way of startup planning and market forecasting. The first part of validated learning is to understand what all things are a waste to our business. We have to check whether the customers are interested in our product or not. To know this we have to release our product quickly into the market and get some real data. Based on this data we can conclude the customer behaviour on our product. This is far better than collecting data via surveys on our product before launching them into the market. In the words of the author- “We must learn what customers really want, not what they say they want or what we think they should want”. Validated learning is the implementation of this statement. The book talks about the need for the freedom of experimentation in a startup. It says that experimentation should be the first version of the product and not just a theoretical enquiry. The customers have to interact with our product as fast as possible because it results in the experimentation of the product and results in getting data regarding valuable customer behaviour. The book talks about the online store Zappos in this regard as an example and how it was founded by Nick Swinmurn. Nick first approached shoe stores and took photos of their inventories and posted them online. And if people showed interest in buying those products he went to the store and bought it and sent it to them. This approach was to check whether there was a demand for shoes online. This was a quicker and more reliable way of finding out the demand of a business rather than methods like customer survey and market hypothesis. Hence validated learning and fast experimentation are very important steps in the Lean startup business model. When the first iteration of our product is launched and distributed we get the knowledge of the customer behaviour and expectations as well as a lot of valuable data regarding the reality of the business.

Steer

Minimum viability product (MVP) is a version of our new product introduced into the market with only the basic features but gets enough attraction from the customers. It helps to get the maximum amount of validated learning about the customers as fast as possible. The lean startup model depends on minimum viable products. Successful companies like Zappos, Dropbox, twitter, etc. are examples of MVPs. They are the best way for Build-Measure-Learn feedback loop. Build-Measure-Learn is the typical way in which a startup develop. A product is first released into the real world, then its success is measured and with this real data the direction of the product for the next stage is determined through validated learning. MVPs can be anything from an advertisement to a prototype. One of the thingg that an entrepreneur need to take care while building an MVP is to remove any feature, process or effort that does not directly contribute to the learning. Another thing is getting an MVP can be challenging because they may look like a below par product and will not show your best ability as an entrepreneur. Here we should remember that an MVP is only the first step towards building a better and highly motivated product according to the Lean Startup model. Also the MVPs can more often give a negative feedback from the customers since it lacks all of the functionalities of a product. But the need for an MVP is to get the valuable customer data for validated learning and for Build-Measure-Learn feedback loop. We should consider it as an input for our next iteration and use it for further innovations in the product. MVP is just the first step of our startup journey.

Measuring success and failures should be optimal as far as a lean startup is considered. Instead of setting milestones or getting ratings from a few customers as a metric for measuring growth, we should have other optimal metrics for measuring success. Two tools for measuring success is introduced in this book: innovation accounting and actionable metrics.

In innovation accounting startups proves the usage of validated learning to develop a startup. In this model startups deliver an MVP to get data on the status of the product. This MVP which gives the customer behaviour, is a real life data for our product’s next step. Every startup shoulf focus towards their ideal objective. Every situation should be focused on reaching that objective. They should be a growth driver. For example if we consider customer activation as a growth parameter, some iteration should focus on improving the user experience on our product- like user interface of an app, smooth onboarding while getting introduced to our service, etc. If such iterations doesn’t increase our customer size, then we should consider it as a failure. We should always be ready to choose whether to persevere on our process or pivot to a new direction. If the drivers are not directing towards achieving our baselines, then it is an indication to pivot.

Actionable metrics is another way startups should measure their success. There are 3 A’s which actionable metrics should follow:

  1. Actionable: A data report is actionable if it indicates clear results, that is, whether a step is a success or a failure.
  2. Accessible: The data reports have to be simple and understandable and very much accessible so that everyone can use them as a source to make further decisions.
  3. Auditable: The data reports should have only the true stats which shows the real customer behaviour and interactions.
As we discussed already a very important decision we have to make at any point is whether to pivot or continue persevering. Even though perseverance is a wonderful quality, for an entrepreneur as far as running a business is concerned, holding onto a failing business after showing too many red flags can be a bad idea. A well-planned pivot in another direction can be helpful to a failing startup to redirect into a successful path. When we talk to entrepreneurs who had pivoted their startup they always tell that they should have pivoted it sooner than later. The reasons for a late pivot can be:
  1. Vanity metrics that make startup entrepreneurs believe that their product is succeeding but rather not, which discourages them from making a pivot.
  2. Unclear hypotheses which disguises that a need for change in a different direction is unnecessary. These hypothesis make us not see failure as it is.
  3. Many people are afraid of a radical change because it means that they are bowing down to failures and it can be discouraging for a team.
The Lean startup model says pivoting as a healthy way of test new approaches for our startup that can lead to better growth and development.

Accelerate

Working with smaller batches is more efficient than larger ones since we can identify and rectify problems much faster and can save a good amount of money and time in that case. The problem with dealing with large batches is that they have to spend more time while releasing each iteration and this is against the principle of less wasting time that a Lean startup has to thoroughly follow. However switching to a smaller batch may feel inefficient if you are a more traditional manager. In the lean startup model, we formulate a hypothesis and deliver an MVP to the market using a small batch in a quicker time. Large batches have the problem of taking more time in our Build-Measure-Learn feedback loop and the validated learning process. Small batches also imply smaller workspaces and inventories which helps to free a good amount of warehouse space. Sustainable growth in a lean startup model is achieved by getting new customers from the activities of old customers. This can be achieved through the following ways:
  • Word of mouth: This is due to the customers being greatly satisfied with our product which results in our product being recommended by them to others.
  • For co-usage of product: This means a product is suggested by a friend so that they can use them together. For example apps like Google Pay is recommended by a friend so that he can send money online.
  • Repeated purchase: Services like netflix where there is a subscription plan so the satisfied customers will keep on renewing their subscriptions.
These sustainable growth forms are helpful for different feedback loops refereed to as “engines of growth”.
  • The “sticky” engine of growth: This focus on the long-term retention of customers to our product. Here two metrics are important- the churn and acquisition rates. The churn rate is the number customers who are not interested in our product anymore. If the churn rate is less than the acquisition rate, our business is growing. Here we need to focus on retaining our existing customers by encouraging them to use our product.
  • The “viral” engine of growth: In this growth model, new customers are acquited from existing customers not by word of mouth but by new customers being exposed to it. Facebook is a good example of it. Here it was not an exhaustive word-of-mouth that popularised facebook, but a fear-of-missing-out phenomenon. Here the customers are using the product on their own and are virally spreading it because of its popularity of use.
  • The “paid” engine of growth: Here the company can either decrease the customer acquisition cost or increase the revenue from present ones. The customer lifetime value is the focus in this case where we find out how much a customer can pay for our product over the period of that products business lifetime. It is better to focus on one engine of growth rather than applying all the three at the same time.

Its not true that companies loose their ability to innovate and grow after they reach a certain size. For faster innovations they need three structural attributes :

  1. Scarce but secure resources which are safe as startups are very sensitive.
  2. Independent development authority for startups for creating and developing new products.
  3. Personal stakesbin the success of products.
When companies reach a certain size they need established managerial systems,here is where "Innovation Sandbox" comes to play, which allows startups full freedom to experiment and innovate . Its very effective as it effects only sandboxed parts .They can see the experiment from start to finish in limited amount of time and also each experiment affects only specified number of customer. Every experiments should be evaluated by 5-10 metrics es which also evaluates the success of the sandbox team. Each team must moniter metrics and customers while tests are in progress and should end when something bad happens. Since the same metrics are used every time its easy to assess if the experiment is a success or not . It also helps to cultivate a solid literacy sbout the same metrics for the whole company. These sandboxes provide rapid iteration , small batches , quick outcomes and constant validated learning but may cause small problems . It takes sometime to see the better results . In old systems problems are harder to find but in the new ones its more obvious. The key is to wait until more innovative approaches become the new normal. So the Lean Startup method will start testing ideas with speed and a good learning process which paves way to success. The age-old problem that employees should work harder to increase productivities should be replaced by a system where employees adopts the Lean startup business method where all assumptions are stated and tested. Speed and quality will increase the increase the value of costomers .Failures will help us learn and speed will prevent unwanted work. The bootomline is that the Lean Startups are models that use continuous innovation to create radically successful businesses.
